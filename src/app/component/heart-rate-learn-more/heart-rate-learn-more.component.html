Heart rate

Heart rate refers to the number of times a person's heart beats per minute (BPM). It is a measure of the heart's activity and indicates how efficiently the heart is pumping blood to the body's organs and tissues. A normal resting heart rate for adults is typically between 60 to 100 beats per minute but can vary based on factors such as age, fitness level, and health status. Exercise, stress, and certain medications can also impact heart rate. Heart rate is typically measured by taking the pulse at the wrist, neck, or chest or using a heart rate monitor device.

Resting Heart Rate

Resting heart rate refers to the number of times a person's heart beats per minute when they are at rest or sitting quietly. It varies from person to person. It is measured after a person has been at rest for at least 5 minutes and has not engaged in any physical activity or consumed caffeine or other stimulants that could affect the heart rate.

Normal Results for Resting Heart Rate:

Newborns 0 to 1-month-old: 70 to 190 beats per minute

Infants 1 to 11 months old: 80 to 160 beats per minute

Children 1 to 2 years old: 80 to 130 beats per minute

Children 3 to 4 years old: 80 to 120 beats per minute

Children 5 to 6 years old: 75 to 115 beats per minute

Children 7 to 9 years old: 70 to 110 beats per minute

Children 10 years and older, and adults (including seniors): 60 to 100 beats per minute

Well-trained athletes: 40 to 60 beats per minute

Causes of changes in Heart Rate

We record the duration of 5 heart rate zones. Each of them is suitable for different types of exercise.

  • Exercise: When the heart rate is at 50%-60% of its maximum capacity, this is suitable for dynamic warm-ups before running and for cool-downs after running.

  • Stress and emotions: Strong emotions, such as anger, depressed mood, anxiety, or excitement, can trigger the release of adrenaline, which can increase heart rate.

  • Medications: Certain drugs could affect the heart's rhythm, such as anesthetics and antibiotics.

  • Health Conditions: Certain medical conditions such as high blood pressure, autoimmune disorders, sleep apnea, etc. may cause heart rhythm problems.

  • Age: Resting heart rate decreases as you get older.

  • Genetics: Some individuals may have naturally higher or lower resting heart rates due to genetic factors.
